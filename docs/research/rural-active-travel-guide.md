# Rural Active Travel England guide → SATN Alignment Option evidence

**Scope.** This note translates the *Rural design guide for active travel* (Active Travel England, 14 July 2026) into evidence that a council-portable Strategic Active Travel Network (SATN) can store and test at the **Alignment Option** stage. It does **not** select routes or produce a design. The guide is technical guidance, not a substitute for statutory process, site surveys, traffic orders, land agreements, consents, equalities assessment, or design assurance. The source publication describes itself as guidance for councils and includes route-creation, land-and-rights and delivery-roadmap appendices. [Publication landing page](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/designing-accessible-rural-active-travel)

**Source convention.** “Official guidance” below is a close paraphrase of ATE. “SATN inference” is a proposed data/rules translation. PDF links are direct official files and page numbers are printed PDF pages.

* [Rural design guide (253 pp)](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/6a58dad2908b618a53702f0e/active-travel-england-rural-design-guide.pdf)
* [Official companion case studies (172 pp)](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/6a58dafc3ae256c99b702f1e/active-travel-england-rural-design-case-studies.pdf)

## Decision: adopt now, adapt into option evidence, defer detailed design

| SATN treatment | Official basis | Machine-checkable Alignment Option evidence | Boundary |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Adopt: optioning before selection** | ATE’s Rural Delivery Roadmap calls for potential alignments, feasibility assessment, and a multi-criteria analysis considering deliverability, cost, safety, attractiveness and directness before shortlisting (guide pp. 21–22; Appendix C pp. 234–35). | Each proposed option for a Network Place pair must have: `alignment_geometry`, `mode_access`, `route_components`, `intervention_archetypes`, `key_users`, `evidence_sources`, `constraints`, `unknowns`, `feasibility_status`, and criterion scores/ratings for `coherent`, `direct`, `safe`, `comfortable`, `attractive`, `deliverability`, `cost_band`, `land_rights_risk`, `environmental_consent_risk`. `selection_rationale` is required only for the selected option. | Scores are transparent triage, not a business case, detailed cost, or engineering approval. |
| **Adopt: connected bidirectional network semantics** | ATE says routes should be coherent, users should understand how route sections form a complete route, and wayfinding is particularly important; rural networks can combine different route types (guide p. 29). | The Community graph is bidirectional. The selected option for every connected Network Place pair must resolve to a continuous chain of canonical nodes and segments; no unexplained discontinuity, direction-only edge, or unlinked component. Require one selected connection per pair. | This is an SATN network-quality test, not a claim that every physical segment is legally available today. |
| **Adopt: directness and everyday destinations** | Routes should be as direct as possible, especially to work, education and public-transport hubs; leisure routes may reasonably give more weight to other principles (guide p. 29). | Record `journey_purpose` (`everyday`, `leisure`, `mixed`) and `directness_basis`. If an option has a material detour, it needs an explicit `directness_tradeoff` (for example safety, accessibility, land availability, ecology or attraction) and supporting evidence. | Do not impose a universal distance/detour threshold from this guide. |
| **Adopt: inclusive design as a network requirement** | ATE says all routes should be accessible to as many users/groups as possible, recommends Equality Impact Assessment, and says schemes must enable PSED compliance (guide pp. 17, 28). | **Adopt: safety includes perceived/social safety** | ATE says actual and perceived safety matter, particularly for women and girls and other marginalised groups; it identifies passive surveillance, escape opportunities and proportionate lighting/ecology trade-offs (guide pp. 29–32). | Require separate `road_safety_risk` and `personal_safety_risk`, plus mitigations and confidence. Options with unknown crossing/traffic exposure or no plausible mitigation cannot be selected. | Lighting design, traffic modelling and formal road-safety audit are later feasibility/design. |
| **Adapt: every segment needs a plausible Intervention Archetype** | At feasibility, ATE says to consider the interventions needed to make each option suitable for objectives and key users, producing different infrastructure levels and a Do Minimum (Appendix C p. 234). | Segment-level `intervention_archetype` is mandatory: e.g. `existing_traffic_free_route_upgrade`, `PRoW_accessibility_upgrade`, `new_off_highway_connection`, `quiet_lane_management`, `access_only_quiet_lane`, `highway_separation`, `crossing_upgrade`, `modal_filter`, `wayfinding_only`, `do_minimum/status_change`. Access-only is a distinct motor-traffic restriction using “Motor vehicles prohibited / Except for Access”; it can preserve property/agricultural access but has definitional/enforcement issues (guide pp. 68–69). | Do not use generic `quiet_lane`. Encode `quiet_lane_designation_or_management` with known/unknown legal status, speed/volume evidence and complementary measures; separately encode `access_only_quiet_lane` with restriction mechanism, access exceptions, enforcement feasibility, servicing/emergency evidence, and traffic-displacement/trial status. ## Minimum Alignment Option record and tests

The following is a portable logical schema, deliberately not a detailed-design schema.

```text
AlignmentOption
  connects: NetworkPlace A ↔ NetworkPlace B
  status: proposed | criticised | selected | rejected | network_gap
  canonical_node_chain: [node_id, ...]
  segments: [{canonical_segment_id, intervention_archetype, access_basis, ...}]
  key_users / journey_purpose / objectives
  evidence: official_guidance + local/source observations + assumptions
  tests: continuity, five-principle ratings, accessibility, safety,
         land-rights, environmental/consent, delivery/maintenance
  feasibility: unknown | early-screened | requires-survey | blocked | viable
  agent_review: proposal, critique, response, selection_reason
```

### Deterministic SATN validations

1. **Pair uniqueness.** Exactly one `selected` option exists for a connected Network Place pair; any competing option remains proposed/rejected with its critique. This implements the project’s one-selected-connection rule while preserving the guide’s multi-option appraisal.
2. **Continuity.** Selected canonical node chains are contiguous, all referenced segments exist, and their union joins both places in both directions. Failure is a rejection or Network Gap—not an implied link.
3. **Intervention completeness.** Every segment has one plausible archetype and a statement of the condition it addresses. `existing route` alone is insufficient: it must say `retain/do minimum` or `upgrade` and why.
4. **Five-principle evidence.** `coherent/direct/safe/comfortable/attractive` must each be `supported`, `plausible_pending_feasibility`, or `unsupported`. A Reviewable Network may expose an `unsupported` result with a blocking Route Refinement Finding; a Complete Network cannot. Safety and accessibility/comfort failures cannot be waived by an agent or human. This translates ATE’s LTN 1/20 principles (guide pp. 28–31), rather than pretending the SATN has verified compliance.
5. **Accessibility safeguards.** Each option records affected user groups and barriers. `access_control = present` requires `essential`, `least_restrictive`, and a mitigation/engagement evidence reference. ATE’s presumption is against access controls on traffic-free routes and says existing restrictive controls should normally be removed (guide pp. 99–100).
6. **Land/rights truthfulness.** A segment with non-public or uncertain access cannot claim `secured`. A selected option with one must be marked `requires_land_rights_feasibility`, name the legal/access mechanism, and retain a fallback or a visible single-point-of-failure risk.
7. **Quiet-lane precision.** `quiet_lane_designation_or_management` and `access_only_quiet_lane` are mutually distinct archetypes. Both require traffic evidence (or an explicit measurement unknown), plus route-specific measures; only the latter requires restriction/enforcement/access-exception data.
8. **Gap discipline.** `network_gap` requires a pair, reason codes (`no_continuous_alignment`, `no_plausible_intervention`, `unresolved_rights`, `unacceptable_safety`, `unresolved_accessibility`, etc.), sources consulted, and next action. It must not contain a selected geometry.

## What should explicitly wait for feasibility/design

Official guidance calls for surveys, stakeholder discussions, ecology/traffic inputs, concept design and appraisal before selection/delivery (guide pp. 21–22; Appendix C pp. 234–35). Therefore the SATN should flag—not resolve—the following:

* traffic speeds, volumes, HGV/servicing, collisions, turning/closure implications, displacement and enforcement;
* detailed cross-section, width, gradient, drainage, surfacing, structures, lighting, junction/crossing and maintenance design;
* PRoW status/width, definitive-map changes, land title/value, landowner agreements, CPO case, licences, planning/environmental/heritage/water consents;
* Equality Impact Assessment, access-control layout and engagement with disabled people, women and girls, equestrians, PRoW officers, emergency services, landowners and ecology stakeholders.

This is not a downgrade of those matters: it prevents an early network model from claiming evidence it does not possess.

## Companion-case-study evidence: useful patterns, not general rules

* **Trial traffic-management options.** ATE’s Exeter Green Lanes case study says ETROs allowed an 18-month trial with monitoring and adjustment; point closures reduced traffic to a level suitable for mixed-traffic cycling and avoided costly protected infrastructure/land acquisition. It also records surfacing, bridge, lighting, access/turning and wayfinding dependencies. **SATN inference:** model `modal_filter` / `access_only_quiet_lane` as testable hypotheses with `trial_candidate`, emergency/servicing constraints and monitoring needs—not guaranteed outcomes. [Case study pp. 41–43](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/6a58dafc3ae256c99b702f1e/active-travel-england-rural-design-case-studies.pdf#page=41)
* **Land fallback and trust.** The Strawberry Line case study reports that prior compulsory-order intent alienated some landowners; negotiated permissive access, diversions and land improvements enabled progress, while lost old-rail alignments needed alternatives and consents. **SATN inference:** retain alternative alignments and record delivery mechanism/relationship risk, rather than treating a disused corridor as automatically available. [Case study pp. 93–95](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/6a58dafc3ae256c99b702f1e/active-travel-england-rural-design-case-studies.pdf#page=93)
* **Accessibility upgrade pattern.** The Trans Pennine Trail case study used a continuous sealed all-weather surface and replaced restrictive barriers with controls retaining authorised maintenance access; ecology licensing delayed part of the work. **SATN inference:** `existing_traffic_free_route_upgrade` is credible only with explicit surface, barrier and ecology/consent checks—rather than treating an off-road line as inherently inclusive. [Case study pp. 105–10](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/6a58dafc3ae256c99b702f1e/active-travel-england-rural-design-case-studies.pdf#page=105)

## Recommended agent workflow

1. **Propose** several evidence-labelled alignment options per desired Network Place pair, using highway, PRoW, traffic-free and new-link mechanisms as ATE envisages (guide p. 22).
2. **Critique** each against continuity, five principles, key-user accessibility, land/rights and unresolved constraints. Preserve unknowns; do not fill them with optimistic defaults.
3. **Select** exactly one option only when it is the best evidence-supported network hypothesis and its feasibility gates are explicit. Store alternatives/rejections and the reason for selection.
4. **Declare Network Gap** if no option survives the minimum tests. Create the next research/feasibility action rather than inventing an alignment.

This matches ATE’s proportionate roadmap and options appraisal while respecting the SATN’s role: a transparent, connected, evidence-aware strategic network, not a premature scheme design.
